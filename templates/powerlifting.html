THE BIG THREE LIFTS

The Squat: King of All Exercises

The barbell back squat is considered by many to be the most effective compound exercise for building overall strength and muscle mass. It primarily targets the quadriceps, hamstrings, and glutes, while also engaging the core and lower back.

Proper Technique

  1. Setup: Position yourself under the bar with it resting across your traps (high bar) or rear delts (low bar). Grip the bar, unrack it, and step back.
  2. Stance: Set your feet slightly wider than shoulder-width apart, toes pointed slightly outward.
  3. Brace: Take a deep breath, brace your core, and keep your chest up.
  4. Descent: Initiate by breaking at the hips and knees simultaneously. Descend until your thighs are at least parallel to the ground.
  5. Bottom Position: Maintain a neutral spine, prevent excessive forward lean, and ensure knees track over your toes.
  6. Ascent: Drive through your heels and midfoot, push your hips forward, and stand up explosively.
  7. Lockout: Fully extend hips and knees without hyperextending your back.

Common Mistakes

  • Knees caving inward (valgus collapse)
  • Heels coming off the ground
  • Excessive forward lean
  • Rounding the lower back
  • Shallow depth (not reaching parallel)
  • Looking down instead of maintaining a neutral neck position

Programming for Strength Gains

  • Frequency: 2-3 times per week for optimal strength gains
  • Volume: 10-15 working sets per week across all sessions
  • Intensity: Mix of heavy (80-90% 1RM) and moderate (70-80% 1RM) work
  • Progression: Add 2.5-5kg/5-10lbs when you can complete all prescribed reps with good form
  • Variations: Front squats, pause squats, and safety bar squats can address weaknesses

The Bench Press: Upper Body Cornerstone

The bench press is the premier upper body strength movement in powerlifting. It primarily targets the chest (pectoralis major), anterior deltoids, and triceps, while also engaging numerous stabilizer muscles.

Proper Technique

  1. Setup: Lie on the bench with your eyes under the bar. Feet flat on the ground, glutes and upper back tight against the bench.
  2. Grip: Grip the bar slightly wider than shoulder-width. For powerlifting, most use a grip where forearms are vertical when the bar touches the chest.
  3. Arch: Create a moderate arch in your lower back while keeping your butt on the bench.
  4. Unrack: Take the bar out with straight arms, don't "lift" it out.
  5. Lower: Lower the bar with control to your lower chest/sternum, maintaining tight shoulders.
  6. Press: Press the bar in a slight diagonal path back toward your face, driving through the whole foot and engaging your lats.
  7. Lockout: Fully extend the elbows without excessive shoulder movement.

Common Mistakes

  • Flaring the elbows too early (increasing shoulder stress)
  • Bouncing the bar off the chest
  • Losing tightness at the bottom position
  • Inconsistent bar path
  • Lifting hips off the bench
  • Uneven lockout (one arm extending faster)

Programming for Strength Gains

  • Frequency: 2-4 times per week for optimal progress
  • Volume: 12-20 working sets per week across all sessions
  • Intensity: Balance heavy (80-90% 1RM), moderate (70-80% 1RM), and lighter (60-70% 1RM) work
  • Progression: Smaller jumps (1-2.5kg/2.5-5lbs) compared to lower body lifts
  • Variations: Close-grip bench, spoto press (paused), floor press, and board press to target sticking points

The Deadlift: Ultimate Strength Test

The deadlift is often considered the purest test of strength - picking up a heavy weight from the ground. It primarily targets the posterior chain: hamstrings, glutes, lower back, and traps, while engaging nearly every muscle in the body as stabilizers.

Proper Technique (Conventional Deadlift)

  1. Setup: Stand with feet hip-width apart, toes straight or slightly turned out, bar over midfoot.
  2. Grip: Hinge at the hips to reach down and grip the bar just outside your legs. Use mixed grip (one overhand, one underhand), hook grip, or straps for heavier lifts.
  3. Starting Position: Drop hips until shins touch the bar, chest up, shoulders slightly in front of the bar, back flat.
  4. Brace: Take a deep breath, create intra-abdominal pressure, and engage lats by "pulling the slack out" of the bar.
  5. Initial Pull: Begin the lift by pushing through the floor with your legs while maintaining a rigid torso angle.
  6. Mid-Range: Once the bar passes the knees, drive hips forward aggressively while maintaining back tightness.
  7. Lockout: Stand tall with shoulders back, glutes squeezed, and knees locked.
  8. Descent: Hinge at the hips first, then bend knees once the bar passes them, maintaining a neutral spine throughout.

Common Mistakes

  • Rounding the lower back (flexion)
  • Starting with the bar too far from the body
  • Jerking the weight off the floor
  • Dropping hips too low (turning it into a squat)
  • Not engaging the lats (allowing the bar to drift forward)
  • Hyperextending at lockout
  • Looking up excessively (cervical hyperextension)

Programming for Strength Gains

  • Frequency: 1-2 times per week (typically less than squat/bench due to higher recovery demands)
  • Volume: 8-12 working sets per week across all sessions
  • Intensity: Mix of heavy singles/doubles/triples (80-90% 1RM) and moderate sets of 4-6 reps (70-80% 1RM)
  • Progression: Add 2.5-5kg/5-10lbs when you can complete all prescribed reps with good form
  • Variations: Sumo deadlift, deficit deadlift, block pulls, Romanian deadlifts to target specific weaknesses

ESSENTIAL POWERLIFTING EQUIPMENT

Basic Equipment

  • Lifting Belt

    Provides core support and increases intra-abdominal pressure. Choose a 10-13mm thick, 4" wide leather or lever belt for maximum support.

  • Wrist Wraps

    Provide wrist support during heavy bench pressing. Stiffer wraps offer more support but less comfort.

  • Lifting Chalk

    Improves grip by absorbing moisture. Essential for heavy deadlifts.

  • Flat Sole Shoes

    Converse Chuck Taylors or dedicated lifting shoes provide a stable platform for deadlifts.

  • Knee Sleeves

    Provide joint warmth and mild support during squats. 7mm neoprene sleeves are standard.

Advanced Equipment

  • Weightlifting Shoes

    Elevated heel helps maintain proper position during squats. Essential for those with limited ankle mobility.

  • Deadlift Socks

    Protect shins from barbell scrapes during deadlifts.

  • Lifting Straps

    Aid grip during high-volume deadlift training (not allowed in competition).

  • Singlet

    Required for powerlifting competitions. Allows judges to see proper depth in squats.

  • Knee Wraps

    Provide significant support and increased rebound in squats (primarily used in equipped divisions).
